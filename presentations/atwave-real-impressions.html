Why this raw CTR is misleading

It mixes machine fetches and human opens in the denominator. The "true" denominator a CPC advertiser cares about is real human opens, which is what we estimate in the next slides.

How segments are classified — click for the device-detection rules

Where the segment tag comes from

The ad server classifies each fetch's device at serve time from user-agent + IP intelligence and writes the resulting deviceId on every event row. The mapping lives in backend/apps/adserver/src/Device/CreateDeviceUsingParser.php:

  • apple proxy — UA is literally Mozilla/5.0 and VPN intel marks the IP as apple + consumer-privacy (Apple MPP infrastructure).
  • apple mail — UA is literally Mozilla/5.0 but the IP is not on Apple's MPP ranges (likely real Apple Mail clients without MPP enabled).
  • google proxy — DeviceDetector identifies UA as "Gmail Image Proxy" (it's a declared bot).
  • yahoo mail proxy — UA prefix YahooMailProxy.
  • desktop / phone & tablet — anything that parses as a real browser UA on a non-proxy IP.

Why this matters

The classification is the foundation of everything in this analysis — we cannot recover the segment after the fact. Once a fetch is misclassified, the corresponding click/open inference goes to the wrong column.

Why this happens

Apple Mail Privacy Protection makes opens unknowable

1 · Prefetch on delivery

Apple's servers download all images for every delivered email, whether or not the user ever opens it — staggered over hours to deliberately obscure timing.

2 · Real opens look identical

When an MPP user does open the email, the images load through the same Apple proxy with the same user-agent. Prefetch and real open are indistinguishable per request.

3 · No client-side signals

Email strips JavaScript and cookies. The only signals that survive: IP, user-agent, timing — and clicks.

Consequence: Apple-segment impression counts measure deliveries × fetch behavior, not human attention. Industry folklore says "40–60% of Apple opens are prefetch" — on this list the real fraction of meaningful Apple impressions is much smaller (next slides).

Finding 2 · The instrument

Clicks can't be faked by proxies — they reveal real readers

Proxies fetch images but never click ads. A click always runs in the user's real browser. Every click carries an impressionId — we joined all 1,323 countable clicks (14d) back to the impression that spawned them, with zero unmatched.

How the click → impression attribution works — click for the join method

The plumbing

Every impression event in emperor.events gets an impressionId at serve time. When a click happens, the click event carries the same impressionId — so the segment of the originating impression is recoverable, even though the click itself always comes from a real browser (the user's, not the proxy's).

Why the cross-store join needs care

A direct JOIN over a 14-day window times out (ClickHouse kills queries at 30s). The pattern that works: pull the click list (~1,300 rows), then probe the impression side with a literal IN-list of impressionIds — hash lookups are cheap. Some clicks land beyond the 14-day window because of the long open tail (Apple MPP staggers prefetch for hours), so we extend the probe by 7 days back.

Method

Estimating real impressions — in plain language

We can't directly see how many Apple-proxy fetches are real opens. But every click comes from a real human. Looking only at readers we can see directly (desktop + phone), we measured that it takes about ~6,700 real opens to produce one desktop click and ~1,200 to produce one phone click. So we count each segment's clicks and multiply back to estimate hidden real opens.

Apple proxy example: 236 desktop clicks × 6,700 + 490 phone clicks × 1,200 ≈ 2.2M real opens vs 40.1M logged fetches → ~5% real. The remaining ~95% are Apple's machines, not people.

① Segment

Every fetch already tagged by who delivered it (Apple proxy / Yahoo / Gmail / real device).

② Anchor

On users we can see directly we have both opens and clicks. That's our reference click-rate.

③ Transfer

For each proxy segment, multiply its clicks by the inverse of the matching real-device rate to estimate hidden opens.

The exact formula, anchors, and caveats — click for technical detail

Formula

real_opens(segment) = clicks_desktop(segment) / CTR_desktop_anchor
                    + clicks_phone(segment)   / CTR_phone_anchor

The multipliers are the inverse of measured direct-reader CTRs: 1 / 0.0150% ≈ 6,700 (desktop), 1 / 0.0822% ≈ 1,200 (phone). Clicks are split by the clicker's device because that's what reveals the user's reading device — the impression segment is the proxy infrastructure, not the user's device.

The core assumption

A human reading a newsletter via an email proxy clicks at the same per-open rate as a directly measured reader on the same device class. This is unverifiable at the request level for Apple MPP — but we can back-check it on Yahoo (next slide), where the proxy fetches only on real opens. Spoiler: the assumption needs a calibration correction.

What kills accuracy

  • Demographic mismatch. If Apple readers have a different per-open click rate than direct readers (e.g. lower, because of MPP UX friction), the multiplier is wrong. Yahoo's data tells us by how much.
  • Click filters off. A scanner click would inflate the estimate. AtWave currently has all click filters disabled. We assume scanner clicks are small (real-browser UAs, residential IPs).
  • Anchor sample size. Only 40 direct-desktop clicks across 14 days. Adds ±31% sampling noise to the desktop multiplier; smaller effect on phone (124 clicks).

The clean subset's 1.10 fetches per (user, blast) is the cleanest "one fetch = one open" signature in the entire dataset. Treating clean-Yahoo as ~100% real opens:

  • Yahoo ground truth: 852K / 1.49M = 58.3% real share
  • Click-anchored estimate: 71.8%
  • Drift: +23% relative overestimate

Why the drift exists

Yahoo's empirical CTR per real open is 266 ÷ 852K = 0.031% — below the pooled direct-reader anchor of 0.040%. Yahoo readers (older, desktop-webmail demographic) click less per open than direct readers, so the multiplier inflates.

Apple has no equivalent anchor

We checked: 99.97% of apple_proxy and 97.6% of apple_mail are on clean fraudType-60 IPs. Apple's MPP infrastructure runs on consumer-privacy ranges that don't trigger fraud classifications, so there's no internal "machine vs human" subset within Apple. The within-Apple fetches/pair ratio is uniform at ~1.17–1.20.

The calibration is window-specific, not time-stable

A 3-month look-back surfaced three discrete step-functions in Yahoo's clean / hosting ratio (Apr 29, May 21, Jun 8–9 — each a platform-level IP-intel reclassification, observed across all 5 clients we tested). So the +23% drift we measured for the June window does not carry forward to other windows: in March (Yahoo ~97% clean) the click-anchored method would have been roughly correct already; in May–June (~55% clean) the +23% holds. The 6.8% headline above is correct for the analysis window; future re-runs must re-derive the calibration from the same window's data. See the platform-shifts tech deck for the detail.

This is why we use the Yahoo drift as our only calibration constant. It's conservative — Apple's demographic likely clicks even less per open than Yahoo's (MPP UX is more passive than webmail), so the +23% may understate the true bias for Apple. The resulting estimate is therefore an upper-end estimate of the truth.

Two things this table reveals

  • The sum across segments (2,340) is 1.77× the unique clicker count (1,326) — the average clicker appears in nearly two impression segments. Same human, multi-device. That ratio is important for designing any per-user policy: deduplicate by userIdentity, not by segment+identity.

Why this is the Option B clincher

At a typical newsletter engagement rate (2–5% click rate per delivered email), this list should produce ~10K–25K clickers per blast, not ~95. The list is either heavily reactivation-fatigued, a low-intent sweepstakes/lead-gen list, or both. With a 90-day look-back and ε ≈ 10% re-inclusion (so users can re-qualify), suppressing the never-engager cohort cuts ad-serving compute by ~90% on this client with zero measurable click-revenue impact — and arguably improves CTR by concentrating delivery on the engaged tail (where the phone-clicker segment is doing real work).

Finding 7 · Infra cost

Every impression is two requests — and that's structural

204M
ad-serving requests per month behind 102M countable impressions

AtWave's template is responsive: every email embeds two different rendered images of the ad — a landscape and a portrait variant. Clients and proxies fetch both, and proxies cache whatever they receive on the first fetch. So the winning ad must be selected and correctly rendered at first serve, for both variants — the decision can't be deferred, and one image can't be reused for both orientations.

What this means for cost

  • The 2× request volume is inherent to responsive email — it cannot be deduplicated away.
  • Savings are limited to making the paired request cheaper to process: the creative decision is already shared (15s decision cache); the remaining headroom is in lightening the second request's pipeline.
  • The meaningful lever is Option B (next slide): users classified as never-engaging get a static response with no selection work at all, while a rotating ε-slice keeps receiving real, correctly matched ads.
Why responsive templates require two ads selected up-front — click for the technical reason

The render path

The email HTML embeds an <img> tag for landscape and another for portrait, each with a unique impression URL. The email client picks which one to render based on viewport width — at render time, not at delivery time. We don't know which the user will see, so we must serve both correctly.

Why we can't pick one and serve the same image for both URLs

The landscape and portrait variants are genuinely different rendered images (different dimensions, layout, copy positioning). Cached as separate URLs by the proxy. The decision pipeline runs once per impression URL — twice per delivered email.

What countableStatus = 2 actually means

The second variant is logged with countableStatus = 2 (RESPONSIVE_LAYOUT_DUPLICATE_VIEW) and is excluded from every aggregate MV's countable filter. So billing and reporting see 102M, but our ad server processed ~204M and ClickHouse stores ~204M event rows. The 2× cost is real and visible only in infra metrics.
