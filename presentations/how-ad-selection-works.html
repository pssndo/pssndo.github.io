How Ad Selection Works

A plain-language guide to how Emperor decides which ad to show when a subscriber opens a newsletter.

0

The Big Picture

When a subscriber opens a newsletter email, every ad slot (called a placement) in that email makes a request to Emperor's ad server. The ad server has a fraction of a second to decide which ad to show.

Think of it like this: The newsletter is a magazine. Each ad slot is a blank space reserved for advertising. When the reader opens the magazine, Emperor instantly fills each blank space with the most appropriate ad it can find.
1

What Emperor Knows About the Request

When the ad slot fires, Emperor receives the following information:

📌

Which Ad Slot

Encoded in the tracking URL embedded in the newsletter. e.g., "Top banner in Sports Daily"

👤

Which Subscriber

Hashed email address from the tracking URL (anonymous, not readable)

📧

Which Newsletter Send

Sendout identifier. e.g., "Sports Daily - Feb 10 edition"

📱

Screen Type

Desktop, Mobile, or Tablet -- determined from a URL parameter

🌎

Location

Looked up from the subscriber's IP address. e.g., "Berlin, Germany"

🔧

Custom Data

Any extra parameters the publisher adds to the URL. e.g., gender, segment, age

Important: Emperor does not receive personal details like name, age, or interests directly from the email provider. The richest user signal is the newsletter content itself -- if someone subscribes to a sports newsletter, they are likely interested in sports.

Email Client Proxies

Many email apps (like Apple Mail and Gmail) load images through their own servers to protect user privacy. This means Emperor sometimes sees the proxy's location and device info instead of the real subscriber's. Emperor knows about these proxy networks and adjusts accordingly -- for example, location data may be less precise for these requests, and fraud checks are tuned to avoid falsely flagging proxy traffic.

Custom Data Builds Up Over Time

When a publisher sends custom data (like interest=sports) in one newsletter and interest=finance in another, Emperor remembers both. Over multiple emails, Emperor builds up a collection of data points for each subscriber. This accumulated data can be used for targeting in future ad requests.

2

Check for Guaranteed Deals (Sponsorships)

Before any competitive auction happens, Emperor checks if there is a sponsorship booked for this ad slot on this date.

What is a sponsorship?

A sponsorship is a pre-sold, guaranteed deal. An advertiser pays a fixed price (e.g., $2,500 for 50,000 impressions) to own an ad slot for a specific period.

Think of it like buying a billboard for a month -- it's yours, no competition.

  • If a sponsorship exists and passes basic checks (right day, right time, right geography), it wins immediately
  • No auction takes place -- the sponsored ad is served
  • Sponsorships are the highest priority in the entire system
Fewer safety checks for sponsorships: Unlike regular campaigns that must pass all 11 eligibility checks (see Step 4), sponsorships skip most of them. They only verify the schedule, location, and whether an ad image is available. Budget limits, frequency caps, pacing, and even fraud checks are all bypassed. The advertiser paid for guaranteed delivery, so Emperor honors that promise -- even if the same subscriber sees the ad many times.

Forced Line Items

Similar to sponsorships, some campaigns can be marked as "forced." These are typically house ads (promoting the publisher's own content) or must-deliver campaigns. Forced campaigns still go through the auction (unlike sponsorships), but they skip budget limits, frequency caps, and pacing -- making them very hard to block. Think of them as VIP guests who still wait in line but get waved through most checkpoints.

3

The Auction (Priority Cascade)

If no sponsorship applies, Emperor runs an auction to decide which ad campaign gets the slot.

How priority works

Every ad campaign (called a line item) has a priority number. Lower numbers = higher priority. Emperor works through priority levels from highest to lowest. It only moves to the next level if no campaign at the current level qualifies.

Priority Levels (lower = more important)

How the auction decides between campaigns at the same priority

Within each priority level, campaigns compete based on their eCPM (effective cost per 1,000 impressions) -- essentially, how much the advertiser is willing to pay.

Direct Campaigns

Use a pre-set eCPM price configured when the campaign is booked.

Programmatic / Exchange

Submit real-time bids via OpenRTB (an industry standard protocol for automated ad buying).

Winner

The higher eCPM/bid wins.

The 95% / 5% fairness rule: Emperor groups campaigns by their bid price and shuffles within each group. 95% of the time, this shuffle stays within price groups (so equal bids get equal chances, but higher bids still win). The other 5% of the time, Emperor does a completely random shuffle -- ignoring bid prices entirely. Why? Without this, a campaign bidding just one cent less than another would almost never win. The 5% random chance prevents any single campaign from permanently dominating a slot by a tiny margin, and gives lower-bidding campaigns occasional impressions so their performance can still be measured.

How Exchange (Programmatic) Bidding Works

When automated ad-buying platforms are in the mix, Emperor doesn't ask them all for bids upfront (that would be slow). Instead, exchange campaigns are included in the priority lineup with an estimated bid. The real bid request only happens when it's the exchange's turn to compete. If the exchange returns a bid that beats the minimum price, it wins. If not, Emperor moves to the next candidate. This saves time by avoiding unnecessary bid requests when a higher-priority campaign would have won anyway.

Weighted rotation (Priority 5 only)

At priority 5, campaigns can be assigned a split delivery percentage. Emperor uses weighted random selection to honor these ratios over time. It works like a raffle wheel -- Campaign A with 50% gets half the wheel, Campaign B with 30% gets a smaller slice, and Campaign C with 20% gets the smallest. Emperor "spins the wheel" for each ad request, and over thousands of spins, the distribution matches the configured percentages.

4

Eligibility Checks (Preconditions)

Before a campaign can win, it must pass a series of 11 eligibility checks. Think of these as filters -- if any filter fails, the campaign is skipped and the next candidate is tried.

The 11 Checks Every Campaign Must Pass

The first campaign to pass all checks wins the slot.

What happens when a check fails? Every time a campaign is skipped, Emperor logs a short code explaining why. This "skipped history" is essential for troubleshooting -- if an advertiser says "my campaign isn't showing," the operations team can look up exactly which check blocked it and how many times. The codes include: DT (wrong day/time), KV (targeting rules didn't match), GEO (wrong location), AUD (audience list mismatch), DEV (wrong device), OBG (campaign budget spent), LBG (line item budget spent), FRD (flagged as fraud), FRQ (subscriber saw it too many times), PAC (pacing says "slow down"), CRE (no ad image available).

More on fraud detection

Emperor runs 18 specialized fraud checks on every request, including: detecting known bots (by their digital fingerprint), flagging VPN and proxy users, scoring IP addresses for suspicious history, catching duplicate or replayed requests, identifying email client proxies (like Apple's privacy protection), and flagging requests from data centers (real people don't browse from server farms). Each check adds to a risk score -- if the total score is too high, the request is rejected.

More on frequency capping

Frequency caps work at three time scales: per hour (e.g., "show this ad at most once per hour"), per day (e.g., "max 3 times per day"), and per lifetime (e.g., "max 10 times total for this entire campaign"). These limits can be set on each campaign individually. If a campaign doesn't have its own limits, it inherits the limits from its parent order (the umbrella group of campaigns). Emperor tracks how many times each subscriber has seen each ad, and these counters automatically reset when their time window expires.

Delivery pacing explained

Pacing controls how fast a campaign delivers its budget over time:

Pacing ModeHow it works
Even Delivery Spreads impressions evenly over the campaign's duration. Emperor compares "how much time has passed" to "how much budget has been spent." If a 10-day campaign has spent 60% of its budget but only 40% of the time has passed, it's ahead of schedule and gets slowed down. If it's behind schedule, delivery speeds up. The slowdown isn't a hard block -- it's a probability. The further ahead of schedule, the less likely each individual ad request will be accepted.
As Soon As Possible No throttling at all. Serves as fast as possible until the budget runs out. Best for short-burst campaigns like flash sales or event promotions where the advertiser wants maximum exposure right away.
Performance Optimization Compares the click-through rate (CTR) on each individual ad slot to the campaign's overall average CTR across all slots. Slots where people click more often get more impressions; slots where people rarely click get fewer. For example, if an ad gets 3.5% clicks in the "Sports Daily" banner but only 0.8% in the "Tech Weekly" sidebar, Emperor will favor the Sports Daily slot. This is the only performance-based optimization in the system today -- it decides where to show ads, but not which ad image to show.

5

Picking the Specific Ad (Creative Selection)

A campaign can have multiple ad creatives (different images, headlines, designs). Once a campaign wins the slot, Emperor needs to pick which creative to show.

Creative types

🎨

Native (Text + Image)

Emperor generates the final ad image by combining the advertiser's image with text (headline, body, call-to-action button) using the publisher's layout template. The publisher controls the visual style -- fonts, colors, layout -- so the ad blends naturally into the newsletter. Each unique combination of ad content + template + screen size produces its own rendered image, stored in cloud storage.

📄

Native (Text Only)

Same as above but with a solid background instead of an advertiser image. Used when the advertiser wants a text-focused ad or doesn't have image assets. Still rendered into an image by Emperor using the publisher's template.

🖼

Display (Banner)

The advertiser provides a finished image. Emperor serves it as-is with no modifications. The image itself may contain text baked into it -- Emperor has no control over or knowledge of the visual content inside display ads.

How a creative is selected

Screen orientation fallback: Each ad creative is designed for a specific screen orientation (portrait or landscape). If no creative matches the exact orientation of the subscriber's screen, Emperor doesn't give up -- it falls back. Portrait requests can use landscape creatives, and mobile requests can fall back to landscape. This prevents showing an empty ad slot just because the campaign only has ads in one orientation.
Creative-level overrides: Individual creatives can optionally override their parent campaign's delivery settings (like pacing mode). So a campaign using "Even Delivery" pacing could have one specific creative that uses "As Soon As Possible" -- allowing fine-grained control at the creative level.
This is effectively random rotation -- all eligible creatives have an equal chance of being shown. There is currently no optimization based on:
  • How well the ad content matches the newsletter content
  • Which creative has the best click-through rate
  • How recently the subscriber has seen a specific creative
  • A/B testing between creative variants
  • Subscriber demographics or interests

This is the gap the planned AI Content Matching Engine aims to fill.

6

Serving the Ad and Tracking

Once the creative is selected, Emperor returns three things:

🖼

The Ad Image

A URL to the pre-rendered image in cloud storage.

👁

Impression Pixel

A tiny invisible image that records the ad was displayed.

🔗

Click Tracking URL

Records the click, then redirects to the advertiser's page.

What happens after serving

  • 15-second decision cache: If the same email has 3 ad slots, they all share one decision cycle -- ensuring consistent ad selection across all slots in one email open. The cache is keyed on the subscriber + newsletter send combination. This also acts as a deduplication safety net: if an email client loads the same image twice (common with preview panes), the second load reuses the cached result instead of counting as a new impression.
  • Frequency counters updated: The subscriber's "times seen this ad" counts are incremented -- separate counters for each time window (hourly, daily, lifetime) and each campaign/order.
  • Impression event queued: The full impression event (subscriber hash, location, device, which ad, which campaign, which creative, timestamp, fraud score) is queued for the analytics database. Events are batched and written in the background so they don't slow down the ad response.
  • Advertiser tracking pixels fired: Any third-party tracking pixels from the advertiser (for their own analytics or verification vendors) are fired in the background after the response is sent.
  • Quality control data sent: If the campaign has quality control enabled, additional verification data is sent to third-party measurement providers. This lets advertisers independently confirm their ads were delivered to real people in the correct locations.
